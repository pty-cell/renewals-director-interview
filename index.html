Candidate Context

Connor is the most operationally aligned candidate. He built the MuleSoft renewals org from scratch, knows the product, and has a strong individual KPI track record (101.7% renewal rate, $250M book). The central question is whether he can step up from managing a team of 6 to setting Director-level strategy and leading through influence at the VP and C-suite level.

1
"You were a founding member of the MuleSoft renewals org and have built it into a $250M book. As a Director, you'd be accountable for vision and strategy — not just execution. Walk me through what you'd prioritize in your first 90 days, and how you'd know whether your approach is working."
Get it Done: Achieve Results Get it Done: Take Accountability
✓ Strong Signal (3–4)
Distinguishes between execution and strategy. Mentions building executive alignment with Sales and CS, reviewing territory pipeline health, identifying systemic gaps, and setting measurable targets for the team — not just himself.
⚠ Weak Signal (1–2)
Focuses on "learning the role" or describes what he already does as Manager. Doesn't articulate how his lens and scope would change at the Director level.
2
"You've had 11 years inside this company. If you stepped into this Director role, what is one significant change you'd push for — in process, strategy, or how the team operates — that you believe leadership might initially resist? How would you make the case for it?"
Courageous Communicator: Demonstrate Courage
✓ Strong Signal (3–4)
Identifies a real, specific tension he's observed — not a generic improvement. Articulates the risk of raising it and shows he'd do it anyway. Has a clear, evidence-based strategy for building the case.
⚠ Weak Signal (1–2)
Names something safe and uncontroversial, or says leadership is already doing everything right. Avoids identifying real friction he's witnessed from the inside.
3
"As a Director, you'd own VP-level relationships with Sales and CS leadership in territory. Tell me about a time you had to convince a senior Sales or CS leader to change how they were approaching a renewal or an at-risk account. What was at stake, what did you do, and what happened?"
Motivate & Champion: Influence
✓ Strong Signal (3–4)
Describes real pushback from a VP or senior stakeholder. Built a compelling case using data, customer insight, or business risk. The outcome demonstrates a clear win or a lesson applied going forward.
⚠ Weak Signal (1–2)
Describes influencing a peer or rep rather than a senior leader. The situation lacked real resistance — the stakeholder was already on board.

Candidate Context

Mike brings serious large-scale leadership credentials — $3B+ revenue at Microsoft, consistent overachievement, and Americas/LATAM coverage. The central question is fit: his background is enterprise support sales, not SaaS renewals, and Salesforce.com experience is a hard requirement on the JD. Probe whether his leadership horsepower outweighs the product and motion gap.

1
"At Microsoft you grew Unified Support from $170M to $240M and accelerated annual revenue growth from 9% to 15%. SaaS renewals operate on a different motion — the primary objective is protecting ARR, managing attrition risk, and maximizing contract value at renewal, not acquiring new logos. How would you translate your growth playbook into that context? What would you do differently, and what would carry over?"
Get it Done: Achieve Results Salesforce Smart: Make Informed Decisions
✓ Strong Signal (3–4)
Shows clear self-awareness about the difference between sales and renewals motions. Articulates specific adjustments — leading indicators of churn vs. pipeline, defending existing value, renewal-specific KPIs. Doesn't just say "retention is a different form of selling."
⚠ Weak Signal (1–2)
Conflates renewals with sales or account management. Can't articulate what he'd need to do differently. Leans on large revenue numbers without engaging with the structural difference in the motion.
2
"You've hired and developed large sales teams at Microsoft and AT&T. Renewal managers need a distinct skill set — negotiating from a position of risk rather than opportunity, managing multi-stakeholder retention plays, and staying focused on KPIs like renewal rate, contract term length, and timely closure. Tell me about a time you developed someone in a capability area that was genuinely new for them — not just coaching them to get better at what they already did."
Motivate & Champion: Coach & Develop
✓ Strong Signal (3–4)
Names a specific person, a specific skill gap, a deliberate development plan, and a measurable outcome. Shows he tailors coaching rather than applying a one-size approach. The skill developed was genuinely new — not incremental improvement.
⚠ Weak Signal (1–2)
Speaks in general terms about "building culture" or "motivating teams." Can't point to a specific individual whose skill profile meaningfully changed as a result of his coaching.
3
"This role is a significant context shift — from Microsoft's enterprise support model into Salesforce MuleSoft SaaS renewals with a different product set, customer base, and operating motion. Tell me about the most significant transition you've navigated in your career and how you got up to speed quickly without losing results in the meantime."
Motivate & Champion: Manage Ambiguity
✓ Strong Signal (3–4)
Describes a real transition with specifics — who they leaned on, how they built credibility quickly, how they balanced learning with delivering. Shows self-awareness about what was genuinely hard and what they had to unlearn.
⚠ Weak Signal (1–2)
Says transitions are easy for them, or describes a low-stakes move. Doesn't reflect on what they had to unlearn or where they initially struggled.

Candidate Context

Perrisa has the deepest MuleSoft commercial expertise of the three — pricing, deal structure, LATAM, and executive stakeholder advisory across 10+ years at Salesforce. The central question is whether she can make the pivot from advisor to owner: her background is commercial governance and deal strategy, not carrying a renewal number with direct accountability for attrition and retention results.

1
"Much of your career has been in deal strategy and commercial advisory — you've enabled and supported renewal outcomes rather than owned them directly. In this Director role, you'd carry the renewal number: renewal rate, attrition, timely closure. Tell me about the closest experience you've had to owning a revenue outcome with your name on it. What did accountability feel like, and how did you respond when results were at risk?"
Get it Done: Take Accountability
✓ Strong Signal (3–4)
Identifies a moment of genuine outcome ownership — not advisory influence. Shows urgency, personal accountability, and a specific response when results were at risk. Doesn't retreat into the framing of "I supported the team that owned it."
⚠ Weak Signal (1–2)
Struggles to name a situation where they were personally on the hook for the result. Defaults to "I influenced the outcome." Can't describe what owning the number felt like when it was under pressure.
2
"You've led deal strategy professionals — people who advise on pricing, structure, and business terms. Renewal managers carry quotas, negotiate directly with customers, and close under time pressure. How would your coaching approach need to change when developing a team with that different profile? Walk me through a specific example of how you've developed someone through a skill set that required them to operate very differently than they had before."
Motivate & Champion: Coach & Develop
✓ Strong Signal (3–4)
Identifies the specific gap between her current team profile and a quota-carrying renewal team. Has a concrete approach to close it. Uses a real person and a real development arc. Shows she's thought about building commercial confidence, not just technical knowledge.
⚠ Weak Signal (1–2)
Assumes her current coaching toolkit translates directly without adjustment. Can't point to developing someone outside their existing comfort zone. Conflates teaching subject-matter knowledge with building execution confidence under pressure.
3
"In your deal strategy and commercial advisory roles, you've had to deliver recommendations that Sales leadership didn't want to hear — commercial guardrails, pricing limits, deal restructures. Tell me about a specific situation where you stood firm on a difficult recommendation with a senior Sales leader, and what happened as a result."
Courageous Communicator: Demonstrate Courage
✓ Strong Signal (3–4)
Describes real friction — a senior stakeholder who pushed back, clear pressure to back down, and a specific rationale for holding the position. The outcome shows business impact, not just a "I was right" moment.
⚠ Weak Signal (1–2)
Describes a soft disagreement that resolved easily, or frames it as the stakeholder "coming around" without real resistance. Speaks abstractly about "setting guardrails" without a specific high-stakes example.
